Preparation

As you begin with this first preparation activity for the course, it is crucial that you understand the importance of preparation. Do not treat these activities as simply a task to check off in as little time as possible. Instead always treat them as opportunities to learn.

Throughout the semester I will assume that you know and understand the concepts from the Prepare activities...unless you tell me differently! The other activities and discussions will be designed to move beyond the readings...give examples and use cases, talk about potential problems, etc. Because of this it is extremely important that you ask for clarification on parts of the readings that didn't make sense. I'm happy to talk about them...but will assume everything is good unless you ask.

Tips

  • Read for understanding not completion. This means that some weeks when the concepts settle quickly in your mind you will fly through the reading. Week one is a good example. It is intended to be mostly review and most of you should get through it fairly quickly. Other weeks you might have to read several times...and spend lots of time working through the code examples until you understand. You may even need to go find a few extra sources on your own for some topics.
  • Dedicate sufficient time! For many weeks the reading will be most of the assigned work for the course. This means that most weeks you ought to plan on 5-6 hours for the reading assignments. For some of you I would expect you will spend most of the time reading and understanding the concepts. For others you may spend most of the time working on the exercises. Play to your strengths and spend your time where it will benefit you and your learning style the most.
  • Don't skip the code examples! Make sure to spend time reviewing these. With the book we are using you can actually interact with the code samples directly. If you have time you should also work through the exercises at the end of each chapter.
  • Take notes! One of the most important things that you can include in your notes are questions that arose in your mind as you were reading. Having this list will help you to know what topics need more time, and will give you something to say when the instructor asks: "Any questions from the reading?"
  • If I didn't assign a chapter in the book it is not because those topics are not important...but often because I expect you to already understand those concepts from a previous course. You may want to read some of the chapters I don't assign if the topics look unfamiliar to you.

  • ...again, don't be afraid to find your own sources...sometimes the way an author explains a concept will not compute with your brain...find another explanation! Here are some additional resources you have free access to as a starting point:

    • Javascript: Novice to Ninja (Sitepoint)
    • You don't know JS
    • Exploring ES6
    • MDN web docs

Reading

  1. Read the Syllabus

  2. Then read through all of Doing Stuff with Web Things. Most of this should be review, but there will be a few new ideas in there for most students. Pay special attention to the parts on Local Storage.

  3. Watch the video Master Mobile UX (32min) on Sitepoint. Be prepared to discuss what you have learned with your classmates.
  4. Turn in your notes (including questions) OR the code from at least one exercise, in ILearn.
